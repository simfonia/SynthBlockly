Synthesizer Types Description

Subtractive vs. Additive Synthesis

These are the two most fundamental approaches to sound design, representing distinct philosophies: Sculpting vs. Stacking.

1. Subtractive Synthesis - Like "Sculpting"

  • Core Logic: Start with more, end with less. Begin with harmonically rich waveforms and use a Filter to cut away unwanted frequencies.
  • Sound: Warm, powerful, classic. Suitable for basic waveforms, PolySynth, etc.

2. Additive Synthesis - Like "Building Blocks"

  • Core Logic: Start with less, end with more. Stack multiple pure Sine Waves to create complex timbres.
  • Sound: Bright, metallic, glassy. Suitable for custom waveform instruments.

Synthesizer Envelope (ADSR)

The envelope defines the volume characteristics of a sound over time. In SynthBlockly, this block must be placed inside an Instrument Container to take effect:

  • A (Attack): The time it takes for the sound to reach peak volume. Low values result in sharp triggers; high values create a fading-in effect.
  • D (Decay): The time it takes to drop from peak to the sustain level.
  • S (Sustain): The volume level (0-1) maintained while a key is held.
  • R (Release): The time it takes for the sound to silence after the key is released.

Characteristics: The purest possible sound, consisting of a single frequency with no overtones.

Sound: Very smooth and round. Ideal for deep sub-bass or pure flute-like tones.

2. Triangle Wave

Characteristics: Contains a few odd harmonics, sitting somewhere between a sine and a square wave.

Sound: Slightly brighter than a sine wave but still mellow. Common for woodwind sounds or soft leads.

3. Square Wave

Characteristics: Rich in odd harmonics, with a distinct "staircase" waveform.

Sound: Hollow and nasal, similar to a clarinet. Frequently heard in retro 8-bit game sounds or thick bass patches.

4. Sawtooth Wave

Characteristics: Contains all integer harmonics, making it the most frequency-rich basic waveform.

Sound: Bright, sharp, and aggressive. The go-to choice for powerful brass, string-like pads, and classic leads.

5. PolySynth (Standard)

Method: Standard Subtractive Synthesis using mixed waveforms by default.

Best for: Basic electric pianos and leads. The best starting point for understanding ADSR.

6. AMSynth (Amplitude Modulation)

Method: Uses one oscillator to rapidly modulate the volume (amplitude) of another.

Characteristics: Has a distinct "shimmering" or "vibrating" quality, often resembling a fast-moving organ.

7. FMSynth (Frequency Modulation)

Method: Uses one oscillator to rapidly modulate the pitch (frequency) of another.

Sound: Complex and bright, with a strong digital or metallic character. Perfect for bells or sharp digital bass.

8. DuoSynth

Method: Layers two independent synthesizer voices to create a thick, rich texture.

Sound: Very thick and powerful. Ideal for grand pads or aggressive lead sounds.

Mathematical & Physical Significance

In "Additive Synthesis", we are essentially "drawing" sound waves using mathematical equations. The core formula is:

y(t) = A1·sin(2π f·k1·t) + A2·sin(2π f·k2·t) + ...

Where f is the base frequency, and kn represents the Frequency Ratio in the blocks.

1. Amplitude (A)

  • Mathematical Meaning: The peak value of the wave. It acts as a multiplier in the equation.
  • Physical Meaning: The amount of energy. In auditory terms, this corresponds directly to Volume. The larger the amplitude, the louder the sound.

2. Frequency Ratio

  • Mathematical Meaning: Controls the density of the wave cycles. fn = Ratio × Base Frequency.
  • Physical Meaning: Corresponds to Pitch.
    • Integer Ratios (1, 2, 3...): Known as "Harmonics", these create pleasing, musical instrument timbres.
    • Non-integer Ratios (e.g., 1.414, 2.73...): Create inharmonic, metallic, or bell-like textures.
